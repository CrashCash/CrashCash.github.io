The motorcycle special tools I've accumulated

These are the motorcycle-specific special tools I've accumulated over the years. This does not include more general purpose tools such as screwdrivers, ratchets, sockets, torque wrenches, DMMs, Allen keys, pliers, breaker bars, combo/box wrenches, taps, dies, drills, drill press, etc.

I have not used some of these tools in 25 years, so digging them up was a trip down memory lane.

I also learned a lot trying to photograph very small black-on-black tools, and setting up lighting/exposure to match the webpage background.

Yamaha YM-01447 fork damper rod holder

Cost: $80

Purpose: Keeps the fork damper cartridge from spinning while you loosen the bottom bolt.

Also known as K&L Tools KL-1223J

Motion Pro 08-0490 43mm fork seal driver

Cost: $43

Purpose: Safely install fork seals.

This is a clever device which splits to fit around the fork leg, then is held firmly together by half-rings so you can push the fork seals in absolutely straight and square without screwing up the lip seal. Also helps put in the big circlips.

Motion Pro 08-0412 ForkTru fork alignment tool

Cost: $27

Purpose: Helps make sure your fork legs are parallel from top to bottom. About as good as the "sight from the side and make sure the far leg vanishes at once behind the close one, instead of gradually" method.

Motion Pro 08-0048 chain alignment tool

Cost: $14

Purpose: Clamp it to your rear sprocket and sight along the rod to check that your rear wheel is aligned.

I've lengthened mine with a 3ft 4mm rod from McMaster-Carr for $10. (A2 tool steel #8116K33)

Suzuki 09940-14940 SV-650 swingarm tool

Cost: $85

Purpose: Allows you to remove/tighten the special castle nut on the end of the swingarm pivot bolt.

Home made FJR-1300 steering ring nut tool

Cost: $75

Purpose: Allows you to loosen or tighten the FJR-1300 triple-tree top ring nut, which is the part that loads the steering bearings.

This is required to perform a very fiddly multi-step procedure to tighten this so it's loaded enough, but doesn't bind.

This was made by Roy Andrews of the FJR forum.

Yamaha YM-01508 slide metal installer

Cost: $100

Purpose: Helps install the middle and top Teflon slide bushings.

Also known as K&L Tools KL-0526K

The 2006-2008 FJR-1300s had three fork bushings instead of the usual two, and you need this tool to install two of them. This was a VERY difficult tool to acquire. I had to go through Yamaha Corporate in California, and I had to file a complaint with the Better Business Bureau to get them to let me order it.

OTC 4743 motorcycle brake pad spreader

Cost: $60

Purpose: Spread the brake caliper pistons without gouging the pad material, and keep them that way while you bleed the fluid. This is actually narrow enough to fit on a bike caliper, as opposed to the automotive ones.

Baja "No Pinch" tire bead tool

Cost: $112

Purpose: This fits in the wheel axle hole and the rack and pinion arrangement forces the tire bead over the lip without damage.

There are several sleeves to fit different axle diameters.

Home made wheel balancer

Cost: $160

Purpose: Stand to hold wheel and axle in ball bearings for static balancing.

Made by a Navy welder on the internet named Rod Neff, it folds up for storage, is adjustable for width and height, and uses common inline-skate bearings.

Motion Pro 08-0427 gear jammer

Cost: $12

Purpose: Insert between gears to hold things from rotating while you tighten/loosen the clutch basket nut.

I bought it for the FJR but it didn't really work so well, as I didn't really know how to use it at the time.

Motion Pro 08-0008 clutch basket holder

Cost: $25

Purpose: Hold the clutch basket steady by engaging the outer gear.

I bought it for the RZ-350 and it worked perfectly. I tried it on the FJR but it didn't fit.

BikeMaster clutch basket holder

Cost: $26

Purpose: Hold the clutch basket steady by engaging the outer gear.

I bought it for the FJR but it didn't fit.

Sears Craftsman 1/3HP 4.5 Amp #315.183010 electric impact wrench

Cost: Expensive. Maybe about $125 30 years ago.

Purpose: I bought this 30 years ago to get the clutch off the RZ-350.

IT IS A BEAST. It weighs about 10lbs and has never met a fastener it couldn't loosen, including the FJR's clutch basket nut. It went BRAAAPPP and job done. It has all the ugga-duggas. I wouldn't part with it at gunpoint.

Harbor Freight hydraulic bike lift

Cost: $220

Purpose: Lift the bike for easy access.

This has a foot pump to raise it, and a pedal to lower it. Rather heavy at 385lbs. I tossed the useless front wheel fixture. Works much better after I drained the cylinder and refilled with quality hydraulic oil.

On-Cycles Cycle-Cat rear stand

Cost: $247

Purpose: Lift the rear wheel.

This is an ingenious design that drops one side to match the lean of the bike to pick it up. Unfortunately the company was an incompetent bunch of dicks who squandered their good idea, priced it far out of reach, had poor customer service, and ran their business into the ground.

Woodcraft front stand

Cost: $180

Purpose: Lift the front of the bike.

This has a pin that slips into the triple-tree pivot. It accepts different size pins as necessary. I do not have a pin to fit the SR, and the FJR's fairing is in the way. It did fit my SV-650s and other bikes.

No-Mar Classic manual tire changing machine

Cost: $610

Purpose: Change tires.

I bought this in an FJR Forum group buy, meaning the very expensive shipping charges were waived.

Blue Eazy-Rizer lift

Cost: $825

Purpose: Lift bike for easy access.

This is a forklift style lift that uses an Acme screw and a drill to lift the bike. It requires a footpeg mount kit specific to the bike. Rather a pain to use.

No-scuff tire bar

Cost: $90

Purpose: Use with No-mar machine to easily install tires.

This is made by David Anderson, who is not associated with No-Mar.

Home made brake fluid supply jar

Cost: $37

Purpose: Supply fluid to master cylinder while bleeding brakes.

I made this out of a Mason jar and computer water cooling parts. This allows true one-man brake bleeding.

Analog compression tester

Cost: $15

Purpose: Test cylinder compression.

This allowed me to tell that the 1985 Suzuki GS550EF only needed new rings and was worth the $200.

M28x1.5 & M18x1.5 thread dies

Cost: $39 & $46

Purpose: Clean threads on FJR front axle and other large items. (Pictured next to a standard size die)

Shorai battery charger

Cost: $85

Purpose: Charge and maintain the Lithium Iron Phosphate (LiFePO4) battery in the FJR.

This is a lightweight battery that does not lose charge when sitting idle. I bought it with the battery and I've never actually needed it. Sigh.

Craftsman #947641 impact driver

Cost: $30

Purpose: Loosen Phillips fasteners.

As you hammer on this tool to loosen the fastener, it simultaneously turns the bit and forces the bit into the fastener so it can't cam out. Great for those '80s-era bike fasteners made out of soft cheese.

This is a quintessential motorcycle tool, in use for the better part of a century.

Gates Krikit belt tension tester

Cost: $25

Purpose: Check drive belt tension on the SR. Rather finicky to use.

RK-Excel chain breaker/riveter

Cost: $100

Purpose: Break a motorcycle drive chain, rivet the rivet-style master links, and press fit the clip-style master links. I decided to go with this as the top-of-the-line tool instead of a cheaper one. I haven't regretted that.

Yamaha YU-33975 Steering ring nut wrench

Cost: $48

Purpose: Allows you to loosen or tighten the RZ-350 triple-tree top ring nut.

Also known as K&L Tools KL-0510F or KL-0201D

PCCC 507 Suzuki shim spanner

Cost: $25

Purpose: Allows replacement of valve shims in the shim-over-bucket design without removing the cams, by pushing down the bucket.

I bought this for the GS1100L

Yamaha YM-01189 (and unknown) flywheel puller

Cost: $15

Purpose: Remove charging rotor from crankshaft.

This threads into the center of the rotor, then you tighten the inner shaft, which pushes on the crankshaft, and pulls the rotor off the Morse taper. I bought this for the RZ-350. I believe the other one was for the Suzuki GS1100L

Home made tire lift

Cost: $65

Purpose: Help lift rear tire into place.

I made this out of semi-trailer load strap and ratchet to hold the SV-650 rear wheel in place while I dealt with the brake caliper and the many bushings and spacers.

Wheeldock wheel chocks

Cost: $265

Purpose: Hold the front wheel of the FJR & SR when I get home to save space in my small garage.

This is an ingenious design that uses a pneumatic arm instead of the standard "tilting bucket" design to hold the wheel. This means there's no need to force the bike over a large hump. You just roll the bike in and step on a pedal. I bought the #2A for the FJR and I was so pleased with the first one that I bought a #3 for the SR, but it doesn't fit as well.

Baxley sport check

Cost: $250

Purpose: Hold the front wheel of the FJR.

This is the standard "tilting bucket" design. It's really well made, but has two problems. First, you have to push the bike over a large hump. Second, the bucket has a tendency to flip up, so the bike bounces off when you try to roll it in. I added springs to stop this.

I used this to hold the FJR on the Harbor Freight lift, and one day, I couldn't see the bucket had reset because of the fairing, and the bike bounced off and away from me. Trying to save it, my hand got pushed into the tool pegboard and I needed several stitches. Fortunately, the FJR came to rest gently on the engine case and was not damaged. I had to fashion a lifting arrangement out of some steel plumbing pipes and ratchet straps to bring it back upright.

That's when I started searching for something better and got the Wheeldocks.
